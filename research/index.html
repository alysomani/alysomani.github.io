The Kids Are All Right: Parental Job Loss and Children’s Academic Performance (JMP) - Coming soon!

This paper investigates the effect of parental layoff on children’s academic achievement in grades 4, 7, and 10, using administrative data from British Columbia. My research design uses a matched comparison as well as the variation in the timing of parental layoffs relative to children’s assessment period in a difference-in-difference setup. I find no significant effects on children’s test scores due to parental job loss. I also find that, even though my matching procedure was successful in matching the layoff sample and the comparison group on observable characteristics, there remains significant negative selection into layoff on unobservables that are correlated with children’s academic ability: children of laid off workers have lower test scores compared to their matched counterparts even when they write tests before the layoff occurs. Mediation analysis suggests controlling on past measures of children’s ability can significantly reduce selection. Additionally, controlling for more refined demographic characteristics and measures of past investment in children can further help reduce selection.



Majority Reaction to Minority Representation in Local Government

This paper studies the racial dynamics of local American politics in a setting with non-partisan elections. I exploit close city council elections in California from 1996 to 2017 to implement a regression discontinuity design, which allows me to study the causal effects of a nonwhite candidate’s victory against a white candidate. I find that in cities where the nonwhite candidate won (“treatment”), compared to cities where the nonwhite candidate lost (“control”), more new white candidates run in the next election. This effect is driven by cities that have gone through bigger demographic changes over the past few decades, which suggests that changes in the racial composition of the city and the associated perception of threat to the dominant status of whites within the city (e.g. Jardina, 2014) are the likely mechanism behind the observed effect.

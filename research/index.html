The Kids Are All Right: Parental Job Loss and Children’s Academic Achievement (JMP)

This paper investigates the effect of parental layoff on children’s academic achievement in grades 4, 7, and 10, using administrative data from British Columbia. I find no significant effects on children’s test scores due to parental job loss. I also find that matching only on parent/household level variables can still lead to selection into layoff on unobservables that are correlated with children’s academic ability.



Majority Reaction to Minority Representation in Local Government

This paper studies the racial dynamics of local American politics in a setting with non-partisan elections. I exploit close city council elections in California from 1996 to 2017 to implement a regression discontinuity design, which allows me to study the causal effects of a nonwhite candidate’s victory against a white candidate. I find that in cities where the nonwhite candidate won (“treatment”), compared to cities where the nonwhite candidate lost (“control”), more white candidates run in the next election. While this effect is partly driven by the presence of more white runners-up in the treatment group, the electoral success of the nonwhite candidate also leads to the entry of new white candidates in the next election. Heterogeneity analysis shows that this effect is driven by cities that have gone through bigger demographic changes over the past few decades, which suggests that changes in the racial composition of the city and the associated perception of threat to the dominant status of whites within the city are an important factor in driving the main result. I do not find evidence that voters are also responding to such a perception of threat. This paper provides new evidence of a political reaction by white citizen-candidates in response to the (nonwhite) identity of the winning candidate.
